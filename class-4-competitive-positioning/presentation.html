Step 9: Identify Your Next 10 Customers

It's easy to say "restaurant owners would want this."

It's harder to NAME 10 restaurant owners who'd buy it.

What Makes a Good Target

  • Match your persona (demographics, psychographics)
  • Have the problem (demonstrated, not assumed)
  • Are accessible (you can reach them NOW)
  • Are early adopter types (willing to try new)
  • Could become paying (have budget and authority)

Customer Hunting Grounds

  • Personal network (friends, family, their connections)
  • LinkedIn and professional networks
  • Industry events and meetups
  • Online communities (Reddit, Discord, forums)
  • Cold outreach (harder but possible)

Start with your three most accessible targets.

Reach out this week.

Step 10: Define Your Core

Your Core

The ONE thing you do better than anyone else that is ALSO hard to copy.

The VRIO Test

V - Valuable

Does the customer care?

R - Rare

Is it uncommon?

I - Inimitable

Is it hard to copy?

O - Organized

Can you leverage it?

All four must be YES for true core.

Features vs Core

Features (NOT Core)

  • "Fast delivery"
  • "Easy to use"
  • "Great customer service"
  • "Best price"

Core (Defensible)

  • "Proprietary logistics network"
  • "Deep UX research methodology"
  • "Community-driven support"
  • "Structural cost advantage"

Features can be copied in 6 months. Core takes years.

The Flywheel Effect

More users ‚Üí Better data ‚Üí Better product ‚Üí More users

Examples: Uber, Google, Facebook

Most startups don't have a core yet.

That's okay. Your job is to build one over time.

Step 11: Chart Your Competitive Position

Two Types of Competitors

Direct: Same solution, same customers

Indirect: Different solution, same problem

Your biggest competitor is often "do nothing."

The status quo. The spreadsheet. The manual workaround.

The 2x2 Positioning Matrix

Pick dimensions that matter to your customer AND differentiate you

Find your white space.

Where no strong competitor exists AND customers want to be served.

Workshop: 30 Minutes

  • Identify 10 potential customers (10 min)
  • Define your core (10 min)
  • Build competitive position chart (10 min)

Remember

  • 10 customers means real names, not abstract segments
  • Core is defensible advantage, not features
  • Everyone has competitors (including "do nothing")
  • Position where customers want to be served

Porter's Five Forces

Michael Porter's framework for analyzing industry competition.

1. Competitive Rivalry: How intense is current competition?
2. Supplier Power: Can suppliers dictate terms?
3. Buyer Power: Can customers drive prices down?
4. Threat of Substitutes: Can customers solve this another way?
5. Threat of New Entrants: How easy is it to enter this market?

Building an Effective Positioning Matrix

The 2x2 matrix is powerful, but only if you choose the right dimensions.

DO:
  • Pick dimensions customers actually care about
  • Choose axes where you can differentiate
  • Use specific, measurable criteria when possible
DON'T:
  • Pick axes just to put yourself in "best" corner
  • Use vague terms like "quality" or "innovation"
  • Ignore indirect competitors and substitutes
